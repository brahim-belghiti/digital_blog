Young people in Morocco often mistake rebellion against local social norms for freedom. But when the form that rebellion takes is imported wholesale from a dominant Western culture — the clothing, the music, the lifestyle — the rebellion is not self-generated. It is a replacement of one external script with another.

Fukuyama's "end of history" claims that Western liberal culture won the clash of civilizations and represents the endpoint of social development. A rebellion that simply adopts that culture does not challenge the claim — it confirms it.

This is not an argument against rejecting local oppression. It is an argument that rebellion without self-reflection and social awareness is what Moroccan darija calls *bessalla* — misguided energy. Freedom understood only as access to sex, alcohol, and drugs is not a philosophy; it is a consumer choice.

The hippie movement illustrates the pattern: it began as anti-conformist, but once it matured, its members converged on shared styles, music, and lexicons. The pioneers became stars others emulated to claim individuality — which defeated the point. Conformity emerges even within anti-conformist movements.

What matters is owning one's subjectivity regardless of which group one is influenced by.
